\begin{abstract}
\textbf{High-Level Summary of Our Project:} This project studies how the U.S. Treasury yield curve is constructed and how different modeling choices affect its shape and interpretation. We were given working code that reproduces the Gurkaynak–Sack–Wright (GSW) zero-coupon Treasury yield curve, which is widely used in academic and policy work. The GSW curve is based on the Nelson–Siegel–Svensson parametric framework. Our main task in this project is to extend the provided code by estimating and comparing alternative yield-curve methodologies that use interpolation rather than parameterization, as surveyed in Waggonner (1997), including spline-based and arbitrage-free approaches used by central banks. In particular, we seek to replicate the approaches outlined by Waggonner: McCulloch (regression splines), Fisher \& Waggonner's own VRP (smoothed spline with roughness penalty). Following a successful replication of these approaches, we compare models in terms of in-sample \& out-of-sample  fits, smoothness, and behavior across maturities.
\end{abstract}

\section{Data Sources}

\textbf{CRSP Treasury Data:}

The CRSP US Treasury Database is a comprehensive source of historical Treasury security data maintained by the Center for Research in Security Prices at the University of Chicago's Booth School of Business. This dataset provides comprehensive historical information on U.S. Treasury securities, including security characteristics, market prices, yields, accrued interest, and duration measures. The database begins in 1961 and is updated regularly, making it one of the most widely used sources for empirical research on Treasury markets. Using the WRDS platform, we accessed the relevant CRSP Treasury files and constructed a dataset suitable for yield curve estimation. These data form the foundation for all curve-fitting methods implemented in this project.

\subsection{Learning Spline Methods \& Matching Waggonner's Results}

Implementing spline-based yield curve methods from scratch proved considerably more challenging than the terse notation in the original papers suggested. Building correct cubic B-spline bases—with properly clamped knot vectors, correct boundary behavior, and numerically stable second-derivative penalty matrices—required working carefully through the spline literature alongside the papers themselves, since the published descriptions leave many implementation details implicit. The Fisher generalized cross-variance (GCV) pipeline (where the lambda parameter is chosen) introduced additional complexity: a naive grid search over lambda values was both slow and unreliable, because the GCV score surface is often flat or multimodal in log-lambda space and the selected lambda can jump discontinuously across dates. Achieving a pipeline that selected lambda efficiently and stably required a two-stage approach—an initial coarse log-spaced grid search to identify a candidate neighborhood, followed by a bounded scalar optimization using Brent's method in log-lambda space—and careful numerical handling of the effective parameters variable that enters the GCV denominator.

\subsection{Methodology}

McCulloch (1975) fits a piecewise cubic spline to the discount function, estimating spline coefficients by ordinary least squares after projecting each bond's cashflows onto the basis. The number and placement of knot points are chosen adaptively from the cross-section of available maturities on each date, with interior knots scaled to the square root of the bond count and placed at equal-count quantiles of the maturity distribution. Fisher, Nychka, and Zervos (1995) take a different approach: they model the instantaneous forward rate curve directly as a cubic B-spline and add a roughness penalty—proportional to the integrated squared curvature of the forward curve—to the pricing objective. The tradeoff between fit and smoothness is governed by a regularization parameter $\lambda$, which our implementation selects automatically for each date by minimizing the Generalized Cross-Validation (GCV) criterion of Fisher et al. using a two-stage grid-plus-refinement search in log-$\lambda$ space.

Waggoner (1997) extends Fisher's smoothed-spline framework by replacing the single scalar $\lambda$ with a Variable Roughness Penalty (VRP): a piecewise-constant function of maturity that applies light smoothing at the short end of the curve (under one year), moderate smoothing over intermediate maturities (one to ten years), and heavy smoothing at long maturities (beyond ten years). This maturity-varying penalty is baked directly into the roughness penalty matrix via numerical quadrature, and the resulting penalized least-squares system is solved once per date without any lambda search. Both the Fisher and Waggoner implementations use the same B-spline node-selection rule and warm-start from the previous date's estimated coefficients to promote temporal stability.

\textbf{Note on Replication Tables:} To evaluate the success of our replication, we compared our estimated results to those reported in the original paper using two key performance metrics: Hit Rate and Weighted Mean Absolute Error (WMAE). Because minor implementation differences, numerical optimization procedures, and sample construction can lead to small deviations from published results, we adopted a tolerance-based approach to assessing replication accuracy. Specifically, we required that at least 90\% of the results across both the in-sample (IS) and out-of-sample (OOS) periods fall within a predefined threshold of the original paper’s values. The thresholds were set at 8% for Hit Rate and 10% for WMAE relative to the published results. Using this criterion, our replication results are broadly consistent with those reported in the paper, indicating that our implementation successfully reproduces the key empirical findings of the original study.

\textbf{Interesting Finding: High Correlation Across Spline-Based Methods} Despite meaningful differences in their construction, the various spline-based yield curve methods produced highly correlated results. The McCulloch regression spline and the Fisher–VRP smoothing spline approach differ in how they control the smoothness of the fitted curve. In particular, the Fisher method includes a roughness penalty parameter that regulates curvature, while the McCulloch spline does not impose an explicit smoothing penalty. Even with these structural differences, the resulting fitted yield curves were extremely similar across most maturities. This suggests that the underlying Treasury price data strongly anchors the shape of the curve regardless of the specific spline methodology used.

\subsection{Lambda Selection in Fisher Curves}

Having constructed the GCV pipeline, it became natural to ask whether the lambda values it selected were behaving sensibly: whether they were stable across time, whether they varied systematically with market conditions, and whether higher or lower smoothing was associated with better or worse model fit. Lambda is not merely a nuisance parameter: it encodes how much data complexity the GCV criterion judges the bond market to exhibit on a given date. As Waggonner highlights in his paper, the choice of lambda represents a trade-off between flexibility on the short end and dampening oscillations on the long end—can we identify any patterns in when this trade-off goes one way or another?

The two lambda tables show a clear pattern in how the model behaves. The decade summary indicates that the 1980s require the most smoothing, with much higher lambda values than other periods. In more recent years (2006 onward), lambda values are generally lower and less variable, suggesting the data require less smoothing overall.

The regime table shows a simple relationship between lambda and model fit. When lambda is low, pricing errors are smaller and a larger share of model prices fall within the bid–ask spread. When lambda is high, errors increase and hit rates decline. This indicates that the model fits the data better on low-lambda dates and struggles more on high-lambda dates.

\textbf{Interesting Finding: Treasury Issuance Patterns and the Long End of the Curve} One interesting observation from the data was that the more recent portion of the sample contains a greater number of long-maturity Treasury securities. This increase in long-term issuance improves the amount of information available for estimating the far end of the yield curve. As a result, curve-fitting procedures tend to produce more stable estimates for long maturities in later years of the sample. Earlier periods contain fewer long-dated bonds, which can make the long end of the curve more difficult to estimate reliably. This difference highlights how, especially when using interpolation methods for curve estimation, the composition of the Treasury market itself can influence yield curve estimation.
